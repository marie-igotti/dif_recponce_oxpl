Colorectal cancer remains a leading cause of cancer mortality, largely due to drug resistance that fuels cancer relapse. In our previous study [10], we characterized the basal transcriptomic differences between oxaliplatin-sensitive and -resistant HCT116 cells, identifying altered redox homeostasis, metabolic adaptation, and reduced proliferative potential as correlates of resistance. However, the comparison of basal gene expression alone was insufficient to distinguish which alterations actively contribute to the resistant phenotype from those that are merely passenger events. In the present study, we directly compared the transcriptional responses of sensitive and resistant cells to oxaliplatin, revealing a fundamentally different adaptive strategy that underlies the resistant phenotype.

### Muted transcriptional response in resistant cells reflects a drug-tolerant persister program

One of the most striking observations was a twofold reduction in the number of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) upon oxaliplatin treatment in resistant HCT116-OX cells compared to their sensitive counterparts. This attenuated transcriptional response is not merely a quantitative difference but reflects a qualitatively distinct cellular state. Growing evidence indicates that chemoresistant cancer cells can enter a drug-tolerant persister (DTP) state characterized by global transcriptional and biosynthetic suppression [1]. Unlike the acute stress response mounted by sensitive cells — which involves massive activation of p53 targets, apoptotic priming, and ribosomal stress — resistant cells appear to have adopted a pre-adapted, "muted" response program that minimizes pro-apoptotic signaling.

This muted response aligns with the concept of "biosynthetic quiescence" described in drug-tolerant persisters, where Myc-dependent transcriptional output is globally suppressed, reducing both proliferative drive and apoptotic priming [1,2]. Importantly, this biosynthetic quiescence is mechanistically distinct from the cell cycle arrest induced by CDK4/6 inhibitors: while both states involve proliferative arrest, only the former confers true chemoresistance through coordinated suppression of anabolic programs [2]. Our observation that resistant HCT116-OX cells mount a narrower transcriptional response while simultaneously activating survival pathways (autophagy, ER protein processing, metabolic rewiring) is entirely consistent with this emerging paradigm of drug-tolerant persistence.

The muted response may also partially reflect the higher basal levels of several key DEGs (ASH1L-AS1, RPRML, TRABD2B, RN7SK, ADAMTS4, GTF2IP4) in resistant cells before treatment, suggesting that resistant cells exist in a "pre-primed" state that requires less transcriptional induction to execute a protective arrest program.

### Accelerated cell cycle arrest as an adaptive survival mechanism

The most prominent functional difference in oxaliplatin response was the rapid and pronounced suppression of cell cycle and mitotic regulators in resistant cells. This suppression encompassed master transcriptional regulators (E2F1, E2F2, FOXM1, MYBL2, MXD3) and their downstream targets, including cyclins (A2, B1, B2, E2), CDC genes, and Aurora kinases (AURKA/B). This transcriptional signature was accompanied by a fivefold reduction in the S-phase population in resistant cells (from 30% to 5%), compared to a twofold reduction in sensitive cells (from 42% to 20%), confirming that the transcriptional suppression translates into a more complete and rapid cell cycle arrest.

At first glance, this finding appears counterintuitive: resistant cells, which ultimately survive oxaliplatin treatment, undergo a more rapid proliferation arrest than sensitive cells. However, this paradox is increasingly recognized as a hallmark of drug-tolerant persister biology. By rapidly exiting the cell cycle upon DNA damage, resistant cells minimize the accumulation of lethal DNA lesions that occur primarily during S-phase when replication forks encounter platinum adducts. This "arrest-to-survive" strategy has been documented across multiple cancer types and chemotherapeutic agents [1,3].

Our findings are particularly consistent with the mechanism described by Cuesta-Borràs et al. [4], who demonstrated that a DPPA3-HIF1α feedback loop enforces a slow-cycling, chemoresistant phenotype in colorectal cancer cells by epigenetically silencing FOXM1 expression. The downregulation of FOXM1 is especially notable in our dataset: FOXM1 is a master regulator of G2/M transition and its transcriptional targets — including PLK1, AURKA, AURKB, CCNB1, and CDC25C — were among the most suppressed genes in oxaliplatin-treated resistant cells. The DPPA3-HIF1α-FOXM1 axis provides a mechanistic framework for understanding how resistant cells rapidly transition from proliferation to quiescence, and the parallels between our transcriptomic signature and the FOXM1-suppressed state described by Cuesta-Borràs et al. are striking [4].

At the level of G1/S control, the E2F family emerged as a central node of differential response. Resistant cells suppressed E2F1, E2F2, and E2F8 upon oxaliplatin treatment, while their expression remained largely unchanged in sensitive cells. This is mechanistically significant because the E2F-Rb axis is the canonical gatekeeper of the G1/S restriction point. The concurrent induction of the CDK inhibitor p21^Waf1^ (CDKN1A) in resistant cells — at the transcript level — further reinforces the transcriptional signature of a rapid, p21-mediated cell cycle exit. This aligns with recent work by Khong et al. [5], who showed that stress granules formed during chemotherapy promote quiescence by enhancing p21 levels and reducing phospho-Rb, thereby driving cells into a protective G0-like state. The elevated p21 transcript levels we observed in resistant cells upon oxaliplatin treatment, occurring alongside E2F target suppression, are consistent with this stress granule-p21-Rb quiescence axis.

Notably, the p21/E2F/Rb circuitry we observed in resistant HCT116-OX cells operates in a p53-proficient context (HCT116 cells are p53 wild-type). This distinguishes our model from many studies of oxaliplatin resistance that focus on p53-mutant settings, where the G1 checkpoint is fundamentally compromised [6,7]. In p53 wild-type cells, p21-mediated G1 arrest can be more rapidly engaged, potentially explaining why HCT116-OX cells execute such an efficient arrest program. Studies in isogenic HCT116 models have previously demonstrated that p21 is essential for oxaliplatin-induced G1 arrest and growth inhibition [8], and our transcriptomic data extend these observations by showing that this p21-dependent arrest program is differentially amplified in the resistant derivative.

The faster cell cycle arrest in resistant cells also finds parallels in the broader drug-tolerant persister literature. Single-cell RNA-sequencing studies have revealed that upon chemotherapy exposure, cancer cells can undergo a rapid transition from proliferative states into a quiescence-senescence continuum, characterized by graded expression of cell cycle inhibitors and progressive suppression of E2F transcriptional output [9]. Our bulk RNA-seq data capture the population-average of what likely represents such a transition — with resistant cells more uniformly and rapidly entering the arrested state. The miR-100-5p/CTDSPL/pRB/E2F1 signaling pathway has also been implicated in cell cycle-mediated chemoresistance in oxaliplatin-resistant CRC cells [10], further supporting the centrality of the Rb-E2F node in the resistant phenotype.

### RN7SK and the ten-gene signature: a convergence on cell cycle control

Among the 440 genes exhibiting differential oxaliplatin response between sensitive and resistant cells, ten showed opposite expression dynamics — upregulated in sensitive HCT116 yet downregulated in resistant HCT116-OX. The directionality of these changes is informative: these genes are induced as part of the stress response in sensitive cells but actively suppressed in resistant cells, suggesting their downregulation is adaptive rather than passive.

Of particular mechanistic interest is RN7SK, which encodes the 7SK small nuclear RNA — a master scaffold of the 7SK snRNP that negatively regulates RNA polymerase II transcription elongation by sequestering the P-TEFb kinase complex [11]. In our data, RN7SK levels dropped to near zero in oxaliplatin-treated resistant cells while accumulating in sensitive cells. This disparity is mechanistically significant because loss of 7SK snRNA specifically suppresses transcription of cell cycle regulators — conditional deletion of Rn7sk in vivo leads to rapid cell cycle exit due to hypersensitivity of cell cycle genes to elongation machinery misregulation [12]. Bandiera et al. [12] demonstrated that cell cycle regulatory genes have high promoter accessibility and low GC-content in gene bodies, making their transcription particularly dependent on proper P-TEFb function. The near-complete loss of RN7SK in oxaliplatin-treated resistant cells thus provides a direct mechanistic link to the observed cell cycle arrest: RN7SK depletion → P-TEFb release → transcriptional pausing dysregulation → selective suppression of cell cycle genes → rapid proliferation arrest.

Furthermore, 7SK has been reported to be downregulated in human tumors, including colorectal cancer [13], and its targeted inhibition promotes gastric cancer cell proliferation and migration [14], suggesting a context-dependent role where 7SK loss can be either tumor-suppressive (in normal/proliferative contexts, by causing inappropriate cell cycle arrest) or tumor-promoting (by enabling survival in the face of genotoxic stress). Our data position RN7SK as a critical node in the differential oxaliplatin response, and to our knowledge, this is the first report of 7SK dynamics in the context of platinum resistance.

The other genes in this ten-gene set further reinforce the cell cycle and oncogenic signaling connection. ASH1L-AS1 is a long non-coding RNA that activates RAS-MAPK signaling via the ASH1L-AS1-ASH1L-NME1 axis [15] and is a known CRC marker [16]. ADAMTS4 encodes a metalloprotease that promotes MAPK signaling and is associated with poor prognosis in CRC [17,18]. RPRML (Reprimo-like) has pro-apoptotic functions via YAP signaling [19]. The coordinated downregulation of these transcripts in resistant cells upon oxaliplatin treatment suggests a concerted suppression of both proliferative signaling (RAS-MAPK) and pro-apoptotic pathways — a dual lock on cell death that contributes to the resistant phenotype.

GNAI1, encoding the Gαi1 inhibitory G protein subunit, showed an inverse expression pattern: upregulated in resistant cells but downregulated in sensitive cells upon oxaliplatin treatment, with a threefold lower baseline expression in resistant cells. Context-dependent roles of GNAI1 have been reported — it suppresses migration and invasion in hepatocellular carcinoma [20] yet reduced GNAI1 expression is linked to colitis-associated tumorigenesis [21] — and its precise contribution to oxaliplatin resistance warrants further investigation.

### Autophagy activation and metabolic reprogramming as complementary survival mechanisms

Beyond cell cycle suppression, our GSEA analysis revealed activation of autophagy-related GO terms (macroautophagy, regulation of autophagy) and metabolic KEGG pathways (propanoate metabolism, valine/leucine/isoleucine degradation, fatty acid degradation) in resistant cells upon oxaliplatin treatment. This is consistent with the growing recognition that autophagy serves as a pro-survival mechanism in drug-tolerant persisters, providing metabolic substrates through self-digestion during the quiescent state [1,22]. The activation of protein processing in the endoplasmic reticulum (KEGG hsa04141) further suggests engagement of the unfolded protein response (UPR), which can promote survival under chemotherapeutic stress.

The activation of SERPINE1 (PAI-1) in resistant cells is particularly notable. SERPINE1 has recently emerged as a key driver of oxaliplatin resistance in CRC. A comprehensive study modelling oxaliplatin resistance in HCT116 cells identified a SERPINE1-based gene signature (RESIST-M) that promotes chemoresistance and metastatic potential through a cholesterol-TGF-β-SERPINE1 signaling axis [23]. In this model, reduced cholesterol biosynthesis in resistant cells leads to TGF-β receptor redistribution out of lipid rafts, sustained TGF-β/SMAD signaling, and SERPINE1 upregulation, which in turn drives EMT and anti-apoptotic programs. Pharmacological inhibition or shRNA knockdown of SERPINE1 re-sensitized resistant cells to oxaliplatin [23]. Additionally, BRD4-mediated PAI-1 expression in tumor-associated macrophages has been shown to drive chemoresistance in CRC [24]. Our finding of SERPINE1 as one of the top DEGs in resistant cells upon oxaliplatin treatment places our results squarely within this emerging SERPINE1-centric model of oxaliplatin resistance.

The metabolic reprogramming we observed — involving fatty acid degradation and amino acid catabolism pathways — aligns with the metabolic flexibility characteristic of drug-tolerant persisters. Unlike proliferating cancer cells that rely on aerobic glycolysis, quiescent persisters often shift toward oxidative phosphorylation and fatty acid oxidation to meet their energetic demands while minimizing ROS production [1,25]. This metabolic shift may contribute to the altered redox homeostasis we previously documented in HCT116-OX cells [10].

### E1A-driven cell cycle reactivation: a strategy to override protective arrest

The rapid and coordinated cell cycle arrest we observed in resistant cells suggested that forced cell cycle reactivation might represent a viable strategy to re-sensitize them to oxaliplatin. This concept — targeting quiescent or slow-cycling cancer cells by reactivating the cell cycle prior to cytotoxic therapy — has gained considerable traction in recent years [3,26,27]. Approaches have included BMP inhibition [28], Wnt pathway modulation [29], and interferon-α exposure [30], all of which can reactivate proliferation in dormant cancer cells. However, each of these strategies carries the risk of merely promoting tumor growth without ensuring subsequent cell death.

Our choice of E1A as a cell cycle reactivator was motivated by its unique dual mechanism: E1A simultaneously drives G1/S transition (by binding Rb and releasing E2F) and primes cells for apoptosis (through p53-dependent and -independent pathways, including Bim upregulation, caspase-2 activation, and NF-κB suppression) [31–35]. This dual activity — forcing cells into S-phase while lowering the apoptotic threshold — is ideally suited for combination with a DNA-damaging agent like oxaliplatin that is most effective against replicating cells. Our data confirm this mechanistic prediction: E1A expression in resistant cells increased the S-phase population from 5.8% to 14% under oxaliplatin treatment while simultaneously increasing the subdiploid (apoptotic) fraction, demonstrating that the reactivated cells are indeed channeled toward death rather than uncontrolled proliferation.

The sensitizing effect of E1A to platinum-based chemotherapy is well-established in preclinical models. E1A expression sensitizes non-small cell lung carcinoma cells to cisplatin through MKP1-mediated mechanisms [36], enhances paclitaxel cytotoxicity in breast and ovarian cancer models [37,38], and synergizes with topoisomerase II inhibitors [39] and histone deacetylase inhibitors [40]. Clinical trials have investigated E1A gene therapy in combination with cisplatin and paclitaxel, demonstrating encouraging response rates [41,42]. A recent comprehensive review further substantiates the mechanism of E1A-dependent cisplatin sensitization through p19ARF/p53 signaling, CtBP corepressor interaction, and HER2/neu downregulation [43]. However, despite decades of promising preclinical and clinical data, E1A-based gene therapy has not yet achieved regulatory approval for clinical use.

Our study provides the first transcriptomic profile of E1A-expressing cancer cells, offering a global view of the transcriptional program underlying E1A-mediated sensitization. The pronounced activation of the "regulation of cell cycle phase transition" pathway (GO:1901987) upon E1A expression in oxaliplatin-treated resistant cells, accompanied by cyclin A, B, and E upregulation and p21 suppression, confirms that E1A overrides the oxaliplatin-induced quiescence program at the transcriptional level. The massive upregulation of p53 (tenfold) in E1A-expressing resistant cells is consistent with E1A's known ability to stabilize p53 through p19ARF-dependent and -independent mechanisms, and likely contributes to the observed apoptotic response.

Importantly, E1A expression alone exerted cytotoxic effects on resistant cells even in the absence of oxaliplatin, as demonstrated by reduced viability, impaired colony formation in soft agar, and increased Annexin V positivity. This inherent cytotoxicity, combined with the chemosensitization effect, supports the potential of pharmacological strategies that mimic E1A's dual activity — cell cycle reactivation coupled with pro-apoptotic signaling — as a therapeutic avenue for oxaliplatin-refractory CRC. Drug repurposing efforts aimed at identifying agents that phenocopy aspects of E1A function (e.g., CDK4/6 inhibitors to modulate Rb-E2F, or p53 activators) may offer a faster path to clinical translation than adenoviral gene therapy [44].

### Limitations and future directions

Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, our transcriptomic analysis captures the population-average response at a single time point (24 h post-treatment). Single-cell RNA-sequencing or time-course experiments would provide higher-resolution insight into the kinetics and heterogeneity of the arrest program. Second, while the HCT116 model is widely used and p53-proficient, it represents a single genetic background; validation in additional CRC lines with diverse mutational profiles (e.g., p53-mutant, KRAS-mutant) would be valuable. Third, the mechanistic link between RN7SK loss and cell cycle gene suppression, while strongly supported by the literature [11,12], remains correlative in our dataset and requires direct experimental validation (e.g., RN7SK overexpression or knockdown studies). Fourth, the E1A experiments were conducted using adenoviral E1A overexpression; pharmacological strategies that mimic specific aspects of E1A function need to be explored for translational feasibility.

Future work should focus on: (i) validating the RN7SK-dependent cell cycle arrest mechanism through gain- and loss-of-function experiments; (ii) exploring whether the ten-gene opposite-dynamics signature has prognostic or predictive value in CRC patient cohorts; (iii) identifying small-molecule compounds that phenocopy E1A's dual cell cycle activation and pro-apoptotic effects for combination with oxaliplatin; and (iv) investigating whether the rapid arrest phenotype generalizes to other DNA-damaging agents (cisplatin, irinotecan) and other CRC subtypes.

### Conclusion

In summary, our integrated transcriptomic analysis reveals that oxaliplatin-resistant HCT116 cells evade platinum toxicity through a coordinated adaptive program involving rapid, transcriptionally enforced cell cycle arrest, concurrent activation of autophagy and metabolic reprogramming, and engagement of pro-survival signaling (SERPINE1). This program is characterized by a muted overall transcriptional response, suppression of the E2F-FOXM1 proliferative axis, and opposite-direction regulation of a ten-gene signature that includes the cell cycle gatekeeper RN7SK. The functional importance of this arrest program is demonstrated by our finding that E1A-driven cell cycle reactivation overrides the protective quiescence and re-sensitizes resistant cells to oxaliplatin, accompanied by p53 activation and apoptotic cell death. These results position cell cycle reactivation — and specifically the E1A-mimetic pharmacological strategy — as a promising avenue for overcoming oxaliplatin resistance in colorectal cancer.
